About Me

I am a fourth-year Ph.D. student in Economics at Rice University in Houston, Texas, USA. Witnessing disparities across vibrant African cultures ignited a deep curiosity about why some societies flourish while others falter. Consequently, my research in development economics primarily explores poverty, culture, religion and social norms on the African continent.

My work delves into individual poverty alleviation and inter-group inequality. It highlights the pitfalls of one-size-fits-all policies in culturally and ethnically diverse regions. I am also analyzing whether advocacy for family planning by local religious leaders can alter social norms rooted within societies.

In addition, I am involved in several collaborative projects. One of these studies examines global poverty dynamics by exploring gender and age disparities over the last 25 years. Another project tries to understand the unique challenges of poverty in Ethiopian refugee communities. Lastly, I am building regional datasets for 26 African countries to study the intricate relationships among poverty, conflict, ethnicity, and religion.

My research utilizes a broad spectrum of data. This includes the World Bank's Living Standards Measurement Study (LSMS), the Demographic and Health Survey, the Afrobarometer, household survey data, current and historical ethnic data, and national data archives from various African countries. Through this work, I aim to provide nuanced, data-backed insights that enhance our understanding of diverse beliefs and traditions across populations.

If you want to know more, feel free to contact me.
